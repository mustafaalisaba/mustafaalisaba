<h2 style="text-align: center;">Death Machines</h2>

<p>
  <span class="initial">W</span>hen he looked into the rearview mirror, he saw madness staring back at him. The thousand-yard stare, lost in the abyss of the pupil. The man began to laugh, but that laughter soon turned into something insane: an agonizing guffaw, laden with fear and despair, as if something had died inside him. He felt an urgent need to escape, to flee from himself. He had loved her for such a short time, and he had lost her forever.
</p>

<p>
  Raymond was a graduate student in contemporary philosophy. Now he had left his studies, his family, and his dreams and plans for life were shattered in the blink of an eye after the death of Lori, a girl from the performing arts faculty whom he had met a month earlier in the university library. They had met while looking for the same book: The Fruit of Nothingness, written by Meister Eckhart, a German theologian from the Middle Ages. At that moment, as they sat leafing through the pages, Lori's gaze pierced him. It was not a look of curiosity, but of familiar tenderness, as if she had known him forever. He could sense it in that instant: there was something beyond her bright, light-colored eyes. It was a warmth and a sparkle within her gaze. The promise of a home and a future together.
</p>

<p>
  Their friendship grew stronger every time they saw each other in the school hallways, the library, or the cafeteria. They talked whenever they could, about anything. The topic of conversation didn't matter; all that mattered was seeing each other, talking, and enjoying each other's company, even with the touch of a kiss on the cheek or a tender hug. After two weeks, Ray asked her out on a date. Lori loved motorcycles. They had a great time at a motorcycle show. He took her hand, and she turned and looked at him with intense eyes. Touching one of her cheeks with his hand, Ray gently pulled her toward him to kiss her. The first kiss was very slow, the two held each other's gaze and smiled, then kissed again, this time more passionately. Nature guided the course of action; nothing could intervene.
</p>
